About Me

Hello everyone! My name is Komal Sharma. I have been living in Canada since 2023 with my family of three. I am originally from Jaipur, India, also known as the "Pink City," a place rich in royal history, culture, and traditions.

I hold a postgraduate degree in Business and Economics along with a Bachelor of Education from India. I began my professional journey in the corporate sector, where I worked for around three years and gained valuable experience in communication, responsibility, and professional growth.

After moving to Canada in 2023 with my family, I transitioned into the field of education. I am currently working as an Educational Assistant in an elementary school, where I have been supporting students for the past 10 months. This role has been a meaningful learning experience for me, helping me grow both personally and professionally while strengthening my passion for working with children and supporting diverse learning needs.

Module 1: Centring Yourself and Building Relationships

Understanding the importance of territorial acknowledgements, Indigenous principles of learning, and meaningful allyship in education.

Takeaway 1: Meaningful Territorial Acknowledgements

Key Learning

The Learning

One of the most significant lessons from Module 1 was understanding the true purpose of territorial acknowledgements (Downie Wenjack Foundation, 2021). Before this module, I viewed them as an opening statement used at meetings or events. I now understand that they are a meaningful way to recognize the original caretakers of the land, acknowledge their ongoing relationship with the land, and encourage reflection on our shared responsibilities. Since I teach at a Sikh Academy in Surrey, British Columbia, I recognize the importance of learning whose traditional territory our school is on and ensuring that any acknowledgement I deliver is sincere and informed rather than simply recited.

Why I Selected This

This learning changed my understanding of land acknowledgements from being a routine practice to an opportunity for genuine respect, learning, and relationship building (Apihtawikosisan, 2016).

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

At the beginning of the school year, I will guide students in learning about the Indigenous Nations connected to the land where our school is located. Together, we will discuss why land acknowledgements matter and create age-appropriate acknowledgements based on understanding rather than memorization.

Checklist for Creating a Meaningful Land Acknowledgement

  • Learn whose traditional territory you are on
  • Pronounce Nation names correctly
  • Understand the history and significance of the land
  • Reflect on why you are acknowledging the land
  • Personalize the acknowledgement with sincerity
  • Consider how your actions support reconciliation beyond your words
  • Continue learning from Indigenous voices and communities

Takeaway 2: The First Peoples Principles of Learning

Framework

The Learning

Learning about the First Peoples Principles of Learning (First Nations Education Steering Committee, n.d.) helped me understand that learning is not only about academic achievement but also about relationships, identity, community, reflection, and connection to the land. These principles encourage educators to value different ways of knowing and to create learning experiences that support the whole child.

Why I Selected This

As an educator, I want my classroom to be a place where every student feels respected, included, and connected. These principles provide a strong foundation for creating meaningful learning experiences (First Nations Education Steering Committee, n.d.).

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

I will include more opportunities for collaborative learning, storytelling, reflection, outdoor learning, and activities that connect students with their own identities and experiences. I also want to create a classroom environment where students feel safe sharing their ideas and learning from one another.

Takeaway 3: Indigenous Allyship

Commitment

The Learning

Another important learning from this module was understanding what it means to be an Indigenous ally (Segal Centre, n.d.). Allyship is not simply saying that I support Indigenous peoples. It requires ongoing learning, listening, reflecting on my own assumptions, and taking meaningful action to support reconciliation in both my personal and professional life.

Why I Selected This

This topic reminded me that reconciliation is an ongoing commitment. As a teacher, I have a responsibility to continue learning and to create respectful learning environments where Indigenous voices and perspectives are represented accurately (Indigenous Foundations, n.d.).

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

I will continue expanding my own knowledge of Indigenous histories, cultures, and perspectives (Indigenous Corporate Training Inc., n.d.). I will choose authentic Indigenous resources whenever possible, encourage respectful discussions, challenge stereotypes if they arise, and ensure Indigenous perspectives are thoughtfully integrated throughout the year rather than only during special events.

Overall Reflection

This module has encouraged a significant shift in my thinking as an educator. I now understand that building respectful relationships begins with understanding my own identity, acknowledging the history of the land where I live and teach, and being open to continuous learning. I also recognize that using respectful terminology, meaningful land acknowledgements, and Indigenous perspectives are all part of creating an inclusive classroom.

Instead of viewing Indigenous education as an additional topic to teach, I now see it as an important perspective that can be naturally woven throughout everyday learning. My goal is to create learning experiences that reflect respect, curiosity, empathy, and inclusion while helping students appreciate the diverse histories and cultures that make up Canada.

It requires humility, listening, and a willingness to continue learning even when conversations are uncomfortable. I understand that I will not know everything, but I can choose to approach this learning with respect, curiosity, and openness.

As an educator, I hope to model these values for my students by creating a classroom where Indigenous histories, cultures, and ways of knowing are respected and where every student feels valued and included.

Module 2: Connections to the Land and Indigenous Worldviews

Takeaway 1: Land as a Living Teacher

Land-Based Learning

The Learning

One important learning from this module was understanding that land is seen as a living teacher in Indigenous worldviews (National Council for Collaborative Indigenous Education, n.d.). Learning does not only happen inside classrooms but also through direct relationship with the land (University of Toronto, n.d.). This helped me understand that knowledge comes from observation, experience, and connection to place, not just from books or formal instruction. In Indigenous worldviews, land is not seen as something separate from people or simply a resource to use, but as a living teacher that holds knowledge, stories, and guidance.

Why I Selected This

I selected this takeaway because it fundamentally shifted my understanding of learning. This challenged my previous idea that learning mainly happens inside classrooms and through textbooks. Instead, I began to understand that learning is relational, experiential, and deeply connected to place.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

As a future educator, I will integrate land-based learning into my teaching practice by taking students outside the classroom whenever possible (National Council for Collaborative Indigenous Education, n.d.). This could include observation walks, nature journaling, seasonal change tracking, or simple reflection activities in nearby parks or school grounds. Even in urban environments, I can encourage students to observe their surroundings closely and think critically about their connection to place. This will help students build curiosity, environmental awareness, and respect for the natural world as an active source of learning.

Takeaway 2: Kinship and Interconnectedness of All Living Beings

Relationships

The Learning

Another important learning from this module was understanding the idea of kinship and interconnectedness (The Walrus, n.d.). Indigenous worldviews teach that all living beings, including animals, plants, water, and land, are connected. This helped me realize that humans are not separate from nature but have a responsibility to care for and maintain balance within these relationships. Indigenous kinship teaches that humans are deeply connected to animals, plants, water, and the land itself.

Why I Selected This

I chose this takeaway because it expanded my understanding of relationships beyond human interactions. This worldview emphasizes responsibility, balance, and respect for all living beings (Nunavut Impact Review Board, n.d.). It helped me reflect on how my actions affect not only people but also the broader environment.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

In my classroom, I will create learning experiences that help students understand interconnectedness (The Walrus, n.d.). I will use activities like circle discussions, storytelling, and reflective writing where students think about how their actions impact others and the environment. I will also establish classroom norms that promote care for shared spaces, materials, and nature. This will help students develop empathy, responsibility, and a stronger sense of community within and beyond the classroom.

Takeaway 3: Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) and Labrador Tea

Indigenous Knowledge

The Learning

Another important learning from this module was understanding Traditional Ecological Knowledge and how it is connected to plants and the land (Downie Wenjack Foundation, n.d.; Yale Environment 360, n.d.). Through my research on Labrador tea, I learned that Indigenous peoples use plants for medicinal purposes while maintaining a respectful and sustainable relationship with nature. This helped me understand that ecological knowledge is based on long-term observation, respect, and reciprocity with the land (Indigenous Climate Hub, n.d.). Sustainability is not a modern idea but a deeply rooted practice based on observation, respect, and reciprocity.

Why I Selected This

I selected this takeaway because my research on Labrador tea helped me connect theory to a real example of Indigenous ecological knowledge. I learned that Indigenous communities have long used plants like Labrador tea for medicinal purposes while maintaining a respectful and sustainable relationship with the land.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

I will integrate TEK into classroom learning by encouraging students to explore local plants and their ecological and cultural significance (National Council for Collaborative Indigenous Education, n.d.). For example, students can research plants found in their community, study their uses, and understand their role in the ecosystem. I would also guide students to observe seasonal plant changes and maintain nature journals. This approach connects science with cultural learning and helps students develop respect for Indigenous knowledge systems and the environment.

Takeaway 4: Comparing Indigenous and Western Worldviews on Land

Worldviews

The Learning

Another important learning from this module was understanding the differences between Indigenous and Western worldviews on land (Yale Environment 360, n.d.). Indigenous perspectives see land as sacred, living, and relational, while Western perspectives often see land as property or a resource to be used (Indigenous Climate Hub, n.d.). This helped me understand how different worldviews shape environmental practices and influence how people respond to climate change and sustainability. Indigenous perspectives emphasize reciprocity, responsibility, and stewardship, while Western European perspectives often focus on ownership and resource extraction.

Why I Selected This

I chose this takeaway because it helped me critically reflect on how different worldviews shape our relationship with land. Understanding this difference helped me see why environmental challenges, such as climate change, are closely linked to worldview and values (Indigenous Climate Hub, n.d.).

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

In my teaching practice, I will encourage students to compare different cultural perspectives on land and environmental care through discussions, case studies, and reflective questions. I will guide students to think about how worldviews influence environmental decisions and climate action. This will help students develop critical thinking, empathy, and global awareness while also understanding the importance of sustainable and respectful relationships with the land (British Columbia Ministry of Education, n.d.).

What Is Your Connection to Land?

My connection to the land has become even more meaningful since becoming a parent. One of the greatest joys for my family is spending time outdoors, whether it's visiting a local park, walking on nature trails, or simply enjoying the fresh air together. My daughter especially loves being outside. She enjoys running, exploring, collecting leaves and flowers, and watching birds and squirrels. I have noticed that if she cannot go outside because of rain or bad weather, she often feels unhappy and misses being in nature. Seeing her excitement reminds me of how important the outdoors is for children's happiness, learning, and overall well-being.

Being on the land gives me a sense of peace and helps me slow down from the busyness of daily life. It is a place where I can connect with my family, appreciate the beauty of nature, and reflect on the importance of caring for the environment. Watching the seasons change and observing plants and animals reminds me that we are all connected to the natural world.

Through this course, I have also come to understand that the land has a much deeper meaning for Indigenous Peoples. It is more than a place to visit—it is a source of identity, culture, history, and knowledge. This perspective has encouraged me to think differently about my relationship with the land and the responsibility we all have to respect and care for it.

As a future teacher, I hope to create opportunities for children to learn outdoors through play, exploration, and observation. I want my students to build meaningful relationships with the land, just as my daughter has, while also helping them understand the importance of respecting nature and learning from Indigenous ways of knowing. I believe that some of the most valuable learning experiences happen outside the classroom, where children can develop curiosity, confidence, and a lifelong appreciation for the natural world.

Overall Reflection

Module 2 deepened my understanding of Indigenous worldviews and the importance of land, kinship, and Traditional Ecological Knowledge in learning and teaching. I learned that land is not just a physical space but a living teacher that holds knowledge, stories, and responsibilities. This shifted my perspective toward a more relational and place-based understanding of education.

A key learning for me was kinship and interconnectedness, where all living beings—including animals, plants, water, and land—are connected and deserve respect. This reminded me of my responsibility as a future educator to help students understand how their actions impact others and the environment, and to value respect, care, and responsibility in learning.

I also learned about Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK), especially through my research on Labrador tea. This showed me how Indigenous knowledge is based on long-term observation, sustainability, and respectful relationships with nature. It helped me understand that Indigenous knowledge is living, relevant, and important in addressing today's environmental challenges.

Another important learning was comparing Indigenous and Western worldviews on land. Indigenous perspectives emphasize relationship and responsibility, while Western perspectives often focus on ownership and resource use. This helped me understand how worldview influences environmental decisions and climate issues.

Overall, this module changed how I view teaching and learning. I now see education as building relationships with land, people, and knowledge systems. As a future educator, I want to create inclusive, land-connected learning experiences that respect Indigenous ways of knowing and support environmental responsibility.

Future Learning Goals

My future learning goal is to continue deepening my understanding of Indigenous worldviews, histories, and ways of knowing in a respectful and ongoing way. I want to learn how to meaningfully integrate Indigenous perspectives and land-based learning into my teaching practice, especially in ways that are authentic and locally relevant.

I also aim to build confidence in facilitating classroom discussions about worldview differences, land, and environmental responsibility in a respectful and inclusive way. Additionally, I want to continue growing as an ally by reflecting on my practice, listening to Indigenous voices, and taking meaningful action in my teaching.
