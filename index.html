About Me

Hello everyone! My name is Komal Sharma. I have been living in Canada since 2023 with my family of three. I am originally from Jaipur, India, also known as the "Pink City," a place rich in royal history, culture, and traditions.

I hold a postgraduate degree in Business and Economics along with a Bachelor of Education from India. I began my professional journey in the corporate sector, where I worked for around three years and gained valuable experience in communication, responsibility, and professional growth.

After moving to Canada in 2023 with my family, I transitioned into the field of education. I am currently working as an Educational Assistant in an elementary school, where I have been supporting students for the past 10 months. This role has been a meaningful learning experience for me, helping me grow both personally and professionally while strengthening my passion for working with children and supporting diverse learning needs.

Module 1: Centring Yourself and Building Relationships

Understanding the importance of territorial acknowledgements, Indigenous principles of learning, and meaningful allyship in education.

Takeaway 1: Meaningful Territorial Acknowledgements

Key Learning

The Learning

One of the most significant lessons from Module 1 was understanding the true purpose of territorial acknowledgements. Before this module, I viewed them as an opening statement used at meetings or events. I now understand that they are a meaningful way to recognize the original caretakers of the land, acknowledge their ongoing relationship with the land, and encourage reflection on our shared responsibilities. Since I teach at a Sikh Academy in Surrey, British Columbia, I recognize the importance of learning whose traditional territory our school is on and ensuring that any acknowledgement I deliver is sincere and informed rather than simply recited.

Why I Selected This

This learning changed my understanding of land acknowledgements from being a routine practice to an opportunity for genuine respect, learning, and relationship building.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

At the beginning of the school year, I will guide students in learning about the Indigenous Nations connected to the land where our school is located. Together, we will discuss why land acknowledgements matter and create age-appropriate acknowledgements based on understanding rather than memorization.

Checklist for Creating a Meaningful Land Acknowledgement

  • Learn whose traditional territory you are on
  • Pronounce Nation names correctly
  • Understand the history and significance of the land
  • Reflect on why you are acknowledging the land
  • Personalize the acknowledgement with sincerity
  • Consider how your actions support reconciliation beyond your words
  • Continue learning from Indigenous voices and communities

Takeaway 2: The First Peoples Principles of Learning

Framework

The Learning

Learning about the First Peoples Principles of Learning helped me understand that learning is not only about academic achievement but also about relationships, identity, community, reflection, and connection to the land. These principles encourage educators to value different ways of knowing and to create learning experiences that support the whole child.

Why I Selected This

As an educator, I want my classroom to be a place where every student feels respected, included, and connected. These principles provide a strong foundation for creating meaningful learning experiences.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

I will include more opportunities for collaborative learning, storytelling, reflection, outdoor learning, and activities that connect students with their own identities and experiences. I also want to create a classroom environment where students feel safe sharing their ideas and learning from one another.

Takeaway 3: Indigenous Allyship

Commitment

The Learning

Another important learning from this module was understanding what it means to be an Indigenous ally. Allyship is not simply saying that I support Indigenous peoples. It requires ongoing learning, listening, reflecting on my own assumptions, and taking meaningful action to support reconciliation in both my personal and professional life.

Why I Selected This

This topic reminded me that reconciliation is an ongoing commitment. As a teacher, I have a responsibility to continue learning and to create respectful learning environments where Indigenous voices and perspectives are represented accurately.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

I will continue expanding my own knowledge of Indigenous histories, cultures, and perspectives. I will choose authentic Indigenous resources whenever possible, encourage respectful discussions, challenge stereotypes if they arise, and ensure Indigenous perspectives are thoughtfully integrated throughout the year rather than only during special events.

Overall Reflection

This module has encouraged a significant shift in my thinking as an educator. I now understand that building respectful relationships begins with understanding my own identity, acknowledging the history of the land where I live and teach, and being open to continuous learning. I also recognize that using respectful terminology, meaningful land acknowledgements, and Indigenous perspectives are all part of creating an inclusive classroom.

Instead of viewing Indigenous education as an additional topic to teach, I now see it as an important perspective that can be naturally woven throughout everyday learning. My goal is to create learning experiences that reflect respect, curiosity, empathy, and inclusion while helping students appreciate the diverse histories and cultures that make up Canada.

Reflection on Land Acknowledgements

One of the most valuable lessons for me was understanding the importance of identity and terminology. I learned that the words we use matter because they reflect respect, recognition, and the right of Indigenous peoples to define themselves. I also gained a better understanding of why using accurate and respectful language is essential in creating inclusive learning environments.

Learning about land acknowledgements also changed my perspective. I had heard land acknowledgements before, but I now understand that they are much more than a formal statement. They are an opportunity to recognize the original caretakers of the land, acknowledge the ongoing relationships Indigenous peoples have with their territories, and reflect on our own responsibilities. This has encouraged me to think about how I can make land acknowledgements more meaningful in my own educational practice.

The First Peoples Principles of Learning were another important part of this module. I appreciate that these principles view learning as holistic, connected to relationships, identity, culture, and the land. They reminded me that every learner brings unique experiences and strengths into the classroom. As an educator, I want to create learning experiences that are inclusive, relationship-based, and respectful of different ways of knowing and learning.

Reflection on Allyship

One of my biggest reflections from this module is that being an ally is a lifelong journey. It requires humility, listening, and a willingness to continue learning even when conversations are uncomfortable. I understand that I will not know everything, but I can choose to approach this learning with respect, curiosity, and openness.

As an educator, I hope to model these values for my students by creating a classroom where Indigenous histories, cultures, and ways of knowing are respected and where every student feels valued and included.
